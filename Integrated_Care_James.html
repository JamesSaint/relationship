Integrated Care

Purpose

This document combines the practical daily plan that Jo can follow that names what Jo takes on so James can heal. Presence first, logistics second. One plan, one list, one calendar.

Working clinical picture
  • Left sided sensory pain and dysaesthesias with occipital involvement.
  • History of right cerebellar infarct and a possible small brainstem lesion.

Care Principles

  • Presence before problem solving. Co regulate first. Speak slow. Keep voice warm and steady.
  • Pacing and prevention. Protect energy early. Front load rest and reduce overstimulation.
  • Safety by design. Remove trip hazards. Plan moves and transitions. Avoid rushing.
  • One thing at a time. Reduce cognitive load. Break tasks into short steps and confirm understanding.
  • Track and adapt. Use the daily log to adjust medications, activities, and triggers.

When James Is in Flare or Crisis

  • Jo reduces verbal engagement and focuses on physical comfort and stability.
  • No new conversations, decisions, or emotional processing during flare periods.
  • Essential communication only: food, medication, reassurance.
  • After flare: gentle debrief, no post-mortem - focus on safety restored.

Cognitive Support

  • Limit screen exposure and multitasking. Use blue-light filters on devices.
  • Break admin work into 15-minute focus blocks with 5-minute rest intervals.
  • Use speech-to-text or have Jo capture notes during symptom spikes.
  • Mark "fog days" in the symptom log and reduce commitments accordingly.

Medication and Monitoring

  • Carbamazepine [Ceased due to intolerance]
  • Keep an updated medication list with doses, start dates, and prescribers. Photograph labels and store in a shared folder.
  • Use a weekly pill box. Prepare each evening for next day. Jo to double check contents against the list.
  • If a dose is missed follow the prescription guidance or phone the pharmacist before doubling.
  • Blood monitoring schedule: Repeat FBC, LFTs, and electrolytes every 3-6 months or per neurologist guidance to track inflammation and drug tolerance.
  • Allergy and sensitivity log: Maintain a record of medication reactions and triggers (e.g., carbamazepine, duloxetine, pregabalin, amitriptyline) for all future prescribers.
  • Neurology coordination: Dr Omar Malik (Cromwell Hospital) is lead consultant. Send all new imaging, medication changes, and reactions via his PA (Natasha) for record integration.
Red flags to escalate the same day
  • New severe headache with neurological change. Face weakness. Speech problem. Vision loss.
  • Allergic type rash or mucosal involvement after medication changes.
  • Persistent vomiting, marked confusion, collapse, or uncontrolled pain.
  • Sudden increase in numbness, imbalance, or vertigo - possible neurological flare.
  • Severe occipital or cranial pain different from baseline - escalate if new pattern emerges.

Emotional Attunement and Presence

  • First contact each day. Eye contact. Warm tone. Short sentences. Ask how his body is right now. Wait for the answer. No fixing yet.
  • Co regulation moments. Sit near. Slow your breath. Offer hand on back or hold his hand. If he nods, stay. If he pulls back, give space without withdrawal.
  • Words that land. I am here. I see you. I am not going anywhere. We will do this one thing at a time.
  • Words to avoid. Should. Why are you not. Any comparison to yesterday as pressure.
  • Repair if tense. Step away for five minutes. Return with a soft apology. Suggest a reset and a glass of water.

Nervous System Regulation Tools

  • Daily grounding: barefoot on grass, slow breath into the belly for 5 to 10 minutes.
  • Body scan three times a day: name sensations without judgment.
  • Weighted blanket or deep-pressure support during high sensory sensitivity.
  • Soft instrumental music or nature sounds during flares (low volume).
  • Jo mirrors slow exhale to co-regulate during spikes.

Home Setup and Safety

  • Clear pathways. No loose rugs. Non slip mats in bathroom. Grab rail if needed.
  • Keep essential items at waist height. Avoid overhead reaching.
  • Good lighting. Night light path to bathroom.
  • Chair with head support for rest periods. Heat packs and topical analgesics in a labelled basket.
  • Emergency card on fridge with NHS number, diagnoses, medications, and contacts.
  • Bedroom temperature 18 to 20Â°C with blackout curtains for sleep quality.
  • Air purifier and low-scent cleaning to reduce histamine and mast cell triggers.
  • Keep emergency medications and antihistamines visible and clearly labeled.

Nutrition and Hydration

  • Three simple meals. Protein at breakfast. Colourful vegetables at lunch and dinner. Light evening meal.
  • Hydration target set each morning. Carafe at desk and bedside.
  • Reduce alcohol. Consider caffeine cut off at 14:00.
  • Prep snack box daily. Nuts, yoghurt, fruit, oat cakes.

Movement and Physio

  • Two short physio blocks daily if tolerated. Gentle neck and shoulder mobility. Nerve glide work as prescribed.
  • Five to ten minute outside walk if stable. Skip if pain spikes or dizziness increases.
  • Stretch before bed. Heat then stretch can reduce night pain.

Coordination and Admin

  • Jo handles calls and forms. Keep a shared calendar and a single folder for letters and reports.
  • Weekly check of repeat prescriptions and delivery timelines.
  • Prepare questions for consultants. Keep a rolling list on the fridge and in Notes.
  • Archive symptom logs weekly and upload to the GitHub /medical folder for consultant review.
  • Track flare triggers (food, stress, sleep loss, overstimulation) with timestamps.
  • Maintain a separate "Energy Map" noting activity vs pain correlation.

Communication and Escalation

  • Jo summarises medical updates weekly and confirms understanding before appointments.
  • If conflict arises, pause and say: "Let's reset for safety."
  • All urgent medical communication is text summarised and logged the same day.
  • For emotional overwhelm: Jo offers grounding, not analysis - "You are safe. Let's slow everything down."

Non Negotiables and Boundaries

  • Jo owns her therapy and family dynamics. No processing with James during flares or at night.
  • All logistics with her ex are handled by Jo. James is not a messenger or shield.
  • Daily rest window is sacred. Phones off. House quiet.
  • If James says stop, everything pauses. Consent and pacing lead.

Words That Help

  • I am here. I can hold this with you.
  • Let me take that from your list. I will handle it and confirm when done.
  • We can do one thing only. Would you like water or rest first.
  • I am closing this loop with my ex now. You do not need to carry it.
  • I want you to receive tonight. No decisions needed.

Words That Harm

  • Why can you not just.
  • You are too sensitive.
  • It is not that bad.
  • Comparisons to her stress load as justification.

Weekly Rhythm

  • One medical admin block. One social connection block. One nature outing if safe.
  • House reset day. Wash bedding. Refill pill box. Update log summary and pain averages.
  • Financial check in. Track costs related to care. File receipts for insurance claims.

How We Know It Is Working

  • James pain spikes reduce in frequency and intensity across two weeks.
  • James sleeps through more nights per week.
  • Fewer arguments. Faster repairs initiated by Jo without prompting.
  • House rhythm feels predictable. Fewer urgent tasks.

When Reciprocity Can Return

Reciprocity can be reintroduced when the above measures hold steady for four weeks and when James feels genuine capacity returning. This is a conversation, not a deadline. The first step will be one small domain handed back with consent.
